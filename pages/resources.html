Featured Study: How Teachers Make Ethical Judgments When Using AI in the Classroom

Summary: A new study from the USC Center for Generative AI and Society reveals that a teacher’s gender and confidence with technology influence whether and how they adopt artificial intelligence in the classroom. The research, which surveyed 248 K-12 educators across the U.S., found that women teachers were more likely to favor rule-based (deontological) ethical approaches, while men focused more on outcomes (consequentialist views). Teachers’ comfort and anxiety with technology also played a significant role in their ethical judgments about AI use.

  • Ethical Frameworks: Teachers differ in their ethical perspectives, with some prioritizing rules and fairness, and others focusing on the consequences of AI use.
  • Technology Confidence: Educators who feel more confident and less anxious about technology are more likely to use AI and make nuanced ethical decisions.
  • Critical Thinking: The study emphasizes the need for educators to guide students in evaluating AI-generated content and to foster critical thinking about when and how to use AI responsibly.
  • Consensus on Ethics: Despite different viewpoints, there is broad agreement among teachers on the need for an ethical framework for AI in education.
  • Promise of Generative AI: The report suggests that, if used thoughtfully, generative AI can enhance learning by supporting brainstorming, critical analysis, and process-based evaluation, rather than simply being seen as a tool for cheating.

How to Use AI in the Classroom Ethically and Responsibly

AI is transforming education by enabling personalized learning, supporting students with learning differences, and streamlining lesson planning and grading. While many teachers see benefits—such as freeing up time for direct student engagement—ethical use is essential. Key concerns include protecting student data privacy, ensuring transparency about data collection, and maintaining fairness in AI-driven assessments. Teachers must remain vigilant about potential biases and errors in AI tools, and balance technology with meaningful human interaction to preserve social and emotional learning.

AI cannot replace the unique mentorship, empathy, and guidance that teachers provide. Instead, it should augment educators, allowing them to focus on creative and critical thinking activities. Teachers also play a crucial role in teaching students digital literacy and ethical AI use, preparing them for a future where AI is a collaborative partner. Responsible integration of AI requires ongoing assessment, clear policies, and a commitment to upholding educational values.
